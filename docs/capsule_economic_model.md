# Capsule Economic Reference Model

## Overview

Digital Lineage Objects (DLOS) enable artifacts to preserve identity,
integrity, and lineage across systems.

Because lineage relationships are embedded directly within capsules,
systems interpreting DLOS can allow value associated with descendant
artifacts to propagate through their ancestry.

This document describes a **reference economic interpretation** of
capsule lineage relationships.

The economic model is not part of the core DLOS protocol.  
Instead, it illustrates one way in which systems built on DLOS may
interpret artifact lineage to route value across creators,
ancestors, and platforms.

---

# Economic Flow

A typical economic event involving a capsule follows the
conceptual flow below.

Revenue Event
│
▼
Lineage Value Flow
│
▼
Ancestor Capsules
│
▼
Platform Margin (≤ 10%)
│
▼
Creator Remainder
│
▼
Creator Allocations


This structure prioritizes:

- recognition of artifact ancestry
- creator primacy
- bounded platform participation

---

# Revenue Events

A revenue event represents any external economic interaction
associated with a capsule.

Examples include:

- artifact sales
- licensing transactions
- distribution payouts
- derivative commercialization
- grants or cultural funding linked to an artifact

The event establishes a **total distributable value** that
may be interpreted by systems implementing capsule economics.

---

# Lineage Value Flow

If the artifact involved in the event is derived from
another capsule, a portion of value may propagate upward
through the artifact’s lineage.

Conceptually:

Descendant Capsule
│
▼
Parent Capsule
│
▼
Earlier Ancestor
│
▼
Origin Capsule


This mechanism acknowledges that new artifacts frequently
emerge through chains of prior contributions.

Lineage value flow allows those contributions to remain
economically visible.

---

# Weighted Lineage Influence

Artifacts closer to the descendant typically exert stronger
influence than distant ancestors.

For this reason, lineage propagation can use a **weighted
generation model** based on lineage distance.

Example conceptual weighting:

Generation Distance

1 → strongest influence
2 → strong influence
3 → moderate influence
4–8 → gradually tapering influence


Beyond a certain generational distance, lineage influence
stabilizes.

---

# Stabilized Lineage Floor

To preserve long-term ancestry recognition, lineage weight
does not decay indefinitely.

Instead, it stabilizes at a minimal participation level.

Example conceptual model:

Early generations → tapered influence
Later generations → stable minimum influence


In many implementations this minimum influence may be
represented as a **1% floor weight**.

This ensures that ancestry remains visible across long
chains of derivatives while keeping the system stable
through normalized distribution.

---

# Normalized Distribution

Lineage allocations occur within a fixed value band
associated with the economic event.

Ancestor weights are normalized against the total
lineage weight.

Conceptually:

Platform Margin ≤ 10%


Platforms may compete within this margin but must never
exceed it.

This constraint prevents extraction-based monopolies
while allowing healthy competition between services.

---

# Creator Remainder

After lineage propagation and platform margin are applied,
the remaining value becomes the **Creator Remainder**.

Conceptually:

Creator Remainder =
Event Value
− Lineage Allocations
− Platform Margin


The creator receives this remainder as the primary
participant in the artifact's economic activity.

This preserves **creator primacy** while still recognizing
ancestral contributions and platform services.

---

# Creator Allocations

The creator may allocate portions of their remainder to
other participants.

Possible allocations include:

- collaborators
- personal beneficiaries
- family lineage
- civic institutions
- cultural preservation initiatives
- community organizations

These allocations occur within the creator domain and do
not alter the structural lineage model.

---

# Protocol Neutrality

The DLOS protocol itself does not mandate any economic
model.

The protocol simply provides the structural primitives
required to express artifact lineage:

- artifact identity
- cryptographic integrity
- parent references
- verifiable ancestry

Economic systems may interpret lineage relationships in
different ways depending on their domain.

Possible implementations include:

- creative industry marketplaces
- research attribution networks
- cultural archives
- educational ecosystems
- decentralized digital marketplaces

---

# Summary

The capsule economic reference model demonstrates how
value associated with digital artifacts may propagate
through lineage while preserving creator primacy and
bounded platform participation.

The model follows three guiding principles:

1. creators remain the primary economic participants
2. artifact ancestry remains visible across derivatives
3. platforms operate within constrained margins

Because lineage relationships are embedded directly within
capsules, systems built on DLOS can interpret artifact
ancestry and enable **lineage-aware value flow** across
generations of work.
