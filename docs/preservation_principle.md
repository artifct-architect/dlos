# Preservation Principle

Human history has repeatedly been lost to war, political upheaval,
institutional collapse, and technological obsolescence.

Across civilizations, archives have burned, records have been altered,
and cultural memory has disappeared alongside the societies that
created it. When records vanish, humanity loses not only information,
but also the continuity of its collective story.

Digital systems were expected to solve this problem, yet most digital
records today remain fragile. Platforms disappear, accounts are lost,
files become unreadable, and centralized systems determine what is
preserved and what is forgotten.

The Digital Lineage Object Standard (DLOS) is built on the belief that
human knowledge and cultural records should not depend on the survival
or authority of any single institution, platform, or political system.

Digital artifacts should be able to carry their own identity,
integrity, and historical lineage so that they remain verifiable
across time, regardless of who controls the surrounding systems.

Preserving history is not merely a technical challenge — it is a
civilizational responsibility.

When records disappear, the past becomes easier to distort,
reinterpret, or erase. Durable digital artifacts provide a way
to protect historical continuity against those forces.

The goal of this work is simple:

to ensure that humanity's knowledge, cultural expression, and
historical record remain verifiable and traceable across generations.

No single authority should be able to silently rewrite or erase the
memory of our species.

Durable digital artifacts make that continuity possible.
