# The Artifact System

Digital Lineage Objects (DLOS) propose a different way of thinking
about digital content.

Instead of treating digital creations as isolated files hosted
inside platforms, the system treats them as artifacts that carry
their own identity, lineage, and historical context.

This document provides a high-level overview of how the artifact
model fits together.

---

## The Problem

The internet was designed around documents.

A URL references a document that contains content such as text,
images, video, or code.

However, the web itself does not understand the objects inside
those documents.

Digital artifacts therefore lose important information over time:

- who created them
- how they evolved
- which works were derived from them
- where they appeared

Most of this information is maintained by platforms rather than
by the artifacts themselves.

When platforms disappear, the history of digital objects often
disappears as well.

---

## The Artifact Model

Digital Lineage Objects introduce the concept of an artifact
capsule.

An artifact capsule contains both the artifact content and
information describing its origin and relationships.

Example capsule structure:

Capsule
│
├ artifact identity
├ creator identity
├ timestamp
├ payload
├ lineage references
└ contextual metadata


This allows the artifact itself to preserve its historical
relationships.

---

## Lineage Networks

Artifacts can reference other artifacts through lineage.

Example structure:

Original Artifact
│
┌────┴────┐
Derivative A Derivative B
│
Derivative C


These connections create a graph of digital creativity.

The artifact network records how ideas evolve through time.

---

## Cultural Artifacts

In the physical world, artifacts preserve history.

Examples include:

- manuscripts
- sculptures
- architecture
- tools

Historians study these objects to understand how societies
develop.

Digital artifacts capable of carrying lineage and context may
serve a similar role in the digital age.

They become containers of cultural memory.

---

## Preservation and Heritage

Artifact systems are particularly relevant for cultural
preservation.

Digital culture currently loses large portions of its history
due to platform shutdowns, lost attribution, and fragmented
archives.

Artifact containers allow digital objects to retain:

- creator attribution
- historical relationships
- contextual documentation

This enables the creation of long-term digital archives.

---

## Spatial Interfaces

Virtual and augmented reality environments provide natural
interfaces for exploring artifact collections.

Instead of browsing lists of files, users may explore:

- virtual museums
- simulation archives
- architectural environments
- historical worlds

Artifacts appear as objects within spatial environments.

---

## Artifact Ecosystems

If artifact systems mature, entire creative ecosystems may
emerge around them.

Artifacts become the fundamental units of digital culture.

Platforms then function as environments where artifacts are
displayed, explored, and expanded.

Possible ecosystem layers include:

Artifact Layer
↓
World Layer
↓
Economic Layer
↓
Cultural Archive


---

## The Long-Term Vision

The artifact model shifts digital culture from a system of
content streams to a system of historical objects.

Instead of disappearing into platform feeds, digital creations
retain identity and lineage.

Over time, this may allow digital networks to function as
a record of how human creativity evolves.

Artifacts become the memory of digital civilization.
